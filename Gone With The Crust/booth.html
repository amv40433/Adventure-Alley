The Contest Booth

The contest booth smelled of sugar, butter, and the unspoken disappointment of a thousand failed pies. Tables lined up like suspects in a lineup, each tin gleaming under the harsh fluorescent lights except for Valerie’s empty, accusatory, impossible to ignore.
I ran my eyes over the empty booth, imagining the scene the night before: pies lined up, judges asleep at their posts, a shadow slipping in and out without a sound. The room was too quiet.
Too sterile.
Like someone had rehearsed the theft, choreographed it, and left nothing behind.
I traced the edges of the table, checking for crumbs, flour dust, anything to tell me the story the thief hadn’t wanted to tell.
Nothing.
A faint smear here, a scuff mark there.
Tiny details, easy to miss, but they were there for someone trained to look.
The kind of details that speak louder than screams.
The hall itself seemed to judge me as I worked.
Every polished floor tile reflected my reflection back at me, a tired, stubborn man chasing shadows.
And I could feel it: the thief had been here, confident, careful, untouchable.
I couldn’t see them, but I could feel the proof left behind in the atmosphere.
A crime, done perfectly, yet imperfect enough for me to start picking apart the lies.
