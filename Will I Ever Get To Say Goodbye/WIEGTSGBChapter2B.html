Chapter 2

As the elevator bell chimed and the doors slid open, I realized I’d been so tangled in my own thoughts that I hadn’t noticed we were getting off on the same floor.

In a moment of sheer panic, I managed to squeak out, “Hey,” my voice sounding braver than I felt. “I think I’ve seen you around here before. Do you live on this floor too?”

He looked over, surprised at first, then smiled—a small, quiet smile that made my chest feel a little too tight.

“Yeah,” he said. “Apartment 3F. You?”

“3B,” I replied. “Guess we’re neighbors.”

“Guess so,” he said, still smiling.

We both stepped out of the elevator, walking in the same direction down the hall.

“I’m Elijah, by the way,” he said as he reached his door.

“I’m Adelyne,” I answered.

“Nice to meet you.”

Then he disappeared inside, and I was left standing in the hallway, the soft click of his door echoing somewhere deep in my chest.

The days after that elevator ride felt… different. Maybe it was nothing, but every time I walked down the hallway, I found myself glancing at 3F, half expecting the door to open.

It didn’t. Not at first.

Then one morning, I saw him again in the mailroom. He was holding a bundle of letters, a half-open envelope caught between his fingers. He looked up when I walked in.

“Hey, 3B,” he said, smiling like we’d been neighbors for years.

I smiled back. “Hey, 3F.”

We talked about the mail constantly getting mixed up, about how the elevator made strange noises at night. He had this calm, steady way of talking, like every word had a soft place to land.

After that, it just… kept happening.

In the laundry room.

The lobby.

The tiny courtyard out back, where the ivy was turning brown.

Always brief. Always easy.

But the more I saw him, the more I wanted to actually know him—beyond the polite neighbor smiles and passing jokes.

One Sunday afternoon, I was lugging my groceries out of the elevator when I heard the click of a door opening. I looked up to see him stepping out of his apartment.

“Hey, stranger,” I joked. “Where are you off to?”

“Just going out for a jog,” he said. “What are you making?” He nodded toward the bags in my arms.

“Broasted chicken and asparagus.” I lifted one bag for emphasis.

We chatted for a bit—light, easy conversation that made time bend in that warm, stupid way crushes always do—before he signaled it was time for him to head out. I knew our little neighborly moment was ending, yet every part of me wanted it to continue.

I wanted to invite him over for dinner. Growing up, my family was always close with our neighbors—people in and out of each other’s houses without a second thought—but this was different. This was an apartment building. This was… him.

And I didn’t want to make anything weird.
